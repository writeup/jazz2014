Toronto's Brass Band Connection

Submitted by Mike DeiCont on Wed Jul 4 3:29am

By Mike DeiCont
 
It was with much excitement that Toronto welcomed two world-renowned brass bands from New Orleans last week, despite having a vibrant traditional jazz sector of its own.  

Toronto’s rich history of such ensembles has lately been pulled to the forefront of the local music scene due the ascendance of The Heavyweights Brass Band which released a well-received debut album and scored several high-profile gigs, including opening twice for hip-hop stars The Roots, since they launched in 2009.

Comprised of trumpet, trombone, tenor sax, sousaphone and drums, the spirited group of recent music school grads brings a youthful energy to the already energetic genre with an extensive repertoire that includes Beyonce, Lady Gaga and Michael Jackson, as well as original and traditional tunes. The popular quintet has invigorated the city’s long established passion for the NOLA sound.

For 33 years, trumpet player Cliff “Kid” Bastien had a residency at Grossman’s Tavern, until his passing in 2003. A student of Crescent City trumpet master Kid Thomas Valentine, Bastien had an in-depth connection with the music. His band, The Happy Pals, now led by trumpet playing Patrick Tevlin, continues to play the traditional New Orleans party music every Saturday at Grossman’s. The venue also hosts “Kid Bastien Forever,” an annual three-day festival in February which celebrates Bastien’s life.

Also showcasing the genre are The Climax Jazz Band, lead by bassist Chris Daniels, which has played at People’s Chicken every Saturday afternoon since 1983, and the Excelsior Jazz Band, fronted by banjo master Syd Calvert, which performs at the Rex Hotel each Sunday.

So it should have been a seasoned crowd that welcomed The Soul Rebels and Trombone Shorty to the TD Toronto Jazz Festival. However, while both acts are still fairly focused on the traditions of brass band music, they have a very contemporary approach to it; consequently their shows attracted a crowd much younger than is usually spotted on the local New Orleans scene.

The powerful Soul Rebels, made up of two trumpets, two trombones, tenor sax, sousaphone and two drummers - each capable of singing lead vocals - performed on June 26th at the Opera House. The crowd was attentive and nodding their heads, but that wasn’t enough energy for the band. Frontman and tenor saxophonist Erion Williams tried to convince them to loosen up. “What happens in the Rebel Zone stays in the Rebel Zone,” he urged. They demonstrated stellar musicianship over the course of their two sets comprised of mostly originals, as well as pop hits, such as "Sweet Dreams" by the Eurythmics and LMFAO’s "Party Rock Anthem". 

The following night, when whiz kid Trombone Shorty and his band Orleans Avenue hit the stage at the Nathan Phillips Square mainstage, the crowd instantly jumped to their feet, and stayed up for the entire show. Commenting on their curfew-less hometown, Shorty encouraged the crowd to stay lively, reassuring, “We can play all night!” The 26-year-old bandleader, who made his professional debut nearly two decades ago, dazzled the audience with a diverse set list that ran from the gems that define the birthplace of jazz to original anthems which blurred the lines between hip-hop, jazz and rock ‘n’ roll.  

“We’re all family,” said Soul Rebels’ trumpet player Marcus Hubbard of his group’s connection with Shorty’s band. The ensembles often share stages and their band members have recorded together. Such camaraderie is not unique to musicians native to New Orleans, said brass-playing-vocalist Marla Dixon who recently relocated to New Orleans from Toronto. “I sure as hell feel it,” she explained. “There are benefits quite often with musicians donating their time and money to help out other musicians with, for example, medical bills.”  

Despite a somewhat revolving membership, the Toronto bands dedicated to Dixieland/New Orleans music manage to harvest a younger generation of players to keep the scene going. Many of those rookies visit NOLA yearly, to perform at French Quarter Festivals, both for their own enjoyment and the learning experiences. That’s a worthy exercise, said Dixon: “You could hear the difference in their playing for a week or two upon their return; there is definitely a different vibe and energy.”

Mike DeiCont is a budding writer, bass player and a recent graduate of Mohawk College’s Applied Music Program.
