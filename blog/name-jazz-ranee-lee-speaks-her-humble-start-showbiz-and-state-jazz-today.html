In the Name of Jazz: Ranee Lee speaks on her humble start in showbiz and the state of jazz today.

Submitted by Jesse Daniel Cole on Tue Jun 25 11:37am

Last Sunday the Jazz Bistro, situated at 251 Victoria Street in Toronto, was the scene of much excitement as one of Canada’s great jazz vocalists, Ranee Lee, took the stage along with her husband and accompanying guitarist Richard Ring.

An Order of Canada recipient and winner of numerous awards both musically and theatrically, Lee is a quadruple threat: an accomplished singer, actress, author, and dancer, as well as a university professor.

Lee found fame, as well as her passion for singing, while portraying the late Billie Holiday in the Dora Mavor Moore Award-winning performance of Lady Day at Emerson’s Bar and Grill in Toronto during the late 1980’s. From that performance onward, Lee’s career continued to grow: to date, she has released 10 albums and performed in both film and theatre, most recently as Mahalia Jackson in The Mahalia Jackson Musical, which debuted at the Segal Centre in Montreal, Quebec, Lee’s current home.

Lee spoke to the TD Jazz News Corps in between her sets at the Jazz Bistro about her rise to fame, her introduction to jazz, and her thoughts on contemporary jazz in Canada and Toronto.

Q: You were born in New York City, how did you end up in Canada and more specifically, Montreal?

A: I was born in Brooklyn, not just New York, but Brooklyn - we’re all so proud of our boroughs. I started my career as a dancer in an Afro-Cuban group - I had three kids and I needed to support them. I then received a contract in Ontario and ended up in Timmons, Ontario, when I was 20 years old. It was there I began my slow transition from dance to song — I found myself wanting to be Dinah Washington, but I had to find my own voice.

Q: How did you come to music as a career choice and jazz in particular?

A: I had dabbled in all kinds of music but to me jazz is the host that everything else branches off from. My husband Richard Ring turned me onto jazz artists like Dizzy Gillespie, Nancy Wilson, and Carmen McRae. There is just so much liberty and freedom in jazz music.

Q: What is the importance of festivals like the TD Toronto Jazz Festival to the Canadian Jazz scene?

A: The TD Toronto Jazz Festival and festivals like it are vital for the exposure they bring to the art form and the exposure they allow to a new audience who may not have discovered jazz. These festivals also allow artists, producers and audiences all to connect and come together with each other. Festivals like this are the key to the lock in the jazz door - they open the way for the sharing and receiving of the art form.

Q: What do you think of the current state of the jazz scene? Is it growing, dwindling?

A: Jazz will never die. The analogy I use is that it’s like a tree going through the seasons: there are times in which the leaves die and fall away and the tree goes to sleep and times when it flourishes and grows. Jazz is like that, it rejuvenates itself and cycles.
