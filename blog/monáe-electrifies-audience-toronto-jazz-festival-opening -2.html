Monáe Electrifies Audience At Toronto Jazz Festival Opening. 

Submitted by Erica Rae Chong on Sat Jun 23 3:53pm

By Erica Rae Chong 

In previous years, the TD Toronto Jazz Festival has banked on iconic acts such as Queen of Soul Aretha Franklin for opening night.  For the 26th edition, programmers took a chance on up-and-coming singer/songwriter Janelle Monáe. 

And the gamble paid off. 

The 26-year-old Kansas City native's distinct blend of rock, pop and funk thrilled a captivated audience, which she brought to their knees - literally.

Monáe enjoyed a burst of popularity in 2010 when her debut album The ArchAndroid (Suites II and III) peaked on the Billboard 200 Charts at #17 and received Grammy nominations for Best Contemporary R&B Album and Best Urban/Alternative Performance (for “Tightrope”). She then went radio silent, but reemerged recently with a guest appearance on Fun’s chart topper “We Are Young". 

Known for futuristic and eccentric music videos, Monáe brought a quirky vibe to a two-hour performance rife with theatrical elements. 

The show opened with three hooded figures at center stage, one eventually unveiled to be Monáe rocking her signature pompadour and black tuxedo. The hooded figures would return later in the concert to help Monáe, wearing snazzy, Cyclops meets T-Pain sunglasses, dramatise a brawl with the two. 

At one point, an easel with a blank canvas was set up and the songstress, draped in a black cape, dramatically swiped and splattered paint across it while singing "Mushrooms & Roses". 

She even surfed the crowd, which she took such command of that towards the end of the evening she was able to have the majority of them crouching or kneeling before her with just a few hand signals.

But the theatrics did not diminish the musicianship. Buoyed by her soulful voice, Monáe and her sure-footed 12-piece band, clad in matching monochromatic outfits, delivered a finely tuned yet organic sound.

The singer acknowledged some of her musical influences, performing a lively version of Prince's “Take Me With U” and a stunning cover of the Jackson Five’s “I Want You Back” in honour of Michael Jackson, whose talent and exuberance she recalled as she bounded around the stage and executed intricate dance moves during “Cold War” and “Tightrope”.

An energetic and confident performer, Monáe's effervescence moved the crowd, which chanted her name and screamed for more. She obliged with a four-song encore after changing into a sharp white tux with black lapels and an undone bowtie draped around her neck. 

Although the petite singer rarely addressed the audience directly, she spoke to them through her music and thrilling stage presence. At the end of the concert, Monáe graciously returned to the stage to introduce the band and, after a quick ID check, bestowed her abstract painting of a woman on a fan celebrating a birthday.

A risky choice has turned out to be a tough act to follow.

Erica Rae Chong is pursing a bachelor’s degree in Journalism at the University of Toronto Scarborough
