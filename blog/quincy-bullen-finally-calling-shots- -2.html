Quincy Bullen: Finally Calling the Shots  

Submitted by Erica Rae Chong on Mon Jun 25 2:50pm

By Erica Rae Chong  

Purists may find the notion of blending techno and jazz sacrilegious, but Quincy Bullen thinks that’s the way of the future. The Markham native, who plays and teaches piano, guitar, bass and drums, will be showcasing his talents at a duo show with saxophonist Rob Christian at Dominion on Queen on June 29 at 8.30pm.

I spoke with the 20-year-old player who released On Q, his debut solo disc of originals, in 2010, about his blossoming career, working with his father — an award winning jazz musician — and how he’s as smooth with the ladies as he is on the ivories.

Q: Who calls the shots when you perform with your father?  

A: It used to be my dad, but now that I’m an adult it’s changed a lot. For example, we had a rehearsal for our Grenada gig; usually I’m under my dad’s coattails whenever I’m in Grenada, but this time it’s going to be my gig, my band, and my father is just there to make sure everything goes smoothly. It’s great that it’s finally my turn, but my father’s so used to being the head honcho and calling the shots that I had to put him in his place! I had to tell him, "That’s not what we’re doing. We’re going to do this." And obviously he wasn’t used to that, but afterwards he told me that he was so happy that I took charge and that he realized that there’s going to be a big change now.

Q: Do you feel pressured to match up to him?  

A: I’m such an easy-going guy it’s hard for me to feel pressure some times. My father is such a great person to talk to, that you don’t realize who he is. He’s such a genuine man that it doesn’t strike you that he’s won a Juno, he’s worked in the Olympics, or he’s performing in Brazil right now. I obviously will try to live up to what my father has done and surpass him, ‘cause that’s exactly what a parent would want.

Q: So, your father is an accomplished musician and producer and your brother plays the drums, what about your mum?  

A: My mum plays the scissors. She’s a hair stylist and she has her own hair salon. She doesn’t actually play an instrument, but she enjoys listening. My mum used to manage bands way back when and she was always kind of in the music industry even though she was not necessarily playing. She is such a huge inspiration to my music and without her I would not be where I am now.

Q: Many people your age are chasing after Lady Gaga and doing covers of Katy Perry on YouTube. What are your thoughts on jazz being an older genre?

A: Oh, definitely jazz is an older genre, but 50 years ago, jazz was hit music. Jazz was the dance! But now dance has evolved into this crazy thing which I believe is just an extension of jazz. Jazz is the art of improvisation, of basing everything on how everyone feels and creating a story in itself. I feel like dance music portrays the same image, except using more frequencies. For instance, you’re limited to the seven white notes and five black notes on jazz piano; those are the notes that we work ourselves around as jazz. But when you get into dance music, you get in between those notes, where it just becomes thousands of frequencies. It’s like you’re trying to paint a picture and now you have more brushes to use.

Q: Do you think jazz will have to reinvent itself to make a comeback?  

A: Yes. I love everything about jazz, but how do I integrate it into the modern feel of what music is now? I’ve been doing a lot of sound design and integrating jazz into something modern. What I want to do is take jazz and bring it to that level of understanding where you can dance to it again. I want to take jazz and modern techno and dance music and create them in harmony with each other.

Q: You perform, you teach and I’ve read that you even act from time to time. What do you do to relax?  

A: I love cooking. The same person that teaches me sound design is also a gourmet chef, so I learn cooking from him as well. I’ve learned to make chinois salad and oxtail, but right now we’re both on a 10-day cleanse - I’ve not eaten anything in nine days.

Q: Anything else? Perhaps hanging out with a girlfriend…  

A: No, unfortunately there is no time for long-term relationships. Even though it’s so hard, I still date and I still go out with people. I love food and fine dining, understanding what I’m eating and taking people out to be treated that way. I’ve been dating sporadically, not necessarily to find the right person, but to balance everything out and to relax.

Erica Rae Chong is pursing a bachelor’s degree in Journalism at the University of Toronto Scarborough
