Me & the Rex: A Jazz Journey!

Submitted by Chaka V. on Sun Jun 23 10:38pm

I have never questioned my love of jazz. I’ve never questioned my jazz preferences. I listen to it every day and simply enjoy it, clumping it all together under one nonspecific label. But this year’s TD Toronto Jazz Festival has got me questioning what jazz is to me, and how where you hear music affects your experience of that music.

I arrived at the Rex Jazz & Blues Bar with my mother (yes, I hang out with my mother). As a child she nurtured my love of jazz. I particularly remember weeks on end when all she would listen to was Miles Davis’ Tutu.

Ever since my first job on Queen Street, I would pass by the Rex. You could always hear great jazz and when I would peek through the window curiously, it always looked like people were having a hell of a good time. But I had never been inside until now.

The Rex is a great venue for jazz and blues events throughout the year. It’s one of those places that keeps both genres alive in Toronto. However, during big bustling festivals like this, it doesn't feel equipped to comfortably allow for jazz fans that are attending a particular show and have no intentions of eating. When we arrived every table and seat was filled and there was little room to stand without getting in the waiters' way. Nevertheless, I was there to see Don Bryon and Michael Occhipinti and later, Victor Lewis with Ryan Oliver. So we found a little corner to stand and prepared ourselves for a good show.

The distinguished-meets-funky looking Don Byron, with his salt and pepper hair, finely plaid red jacket, black polka-dot scarf, white spectacles and blue jeans, exemplifies cool.

The clarinet and saxophone player (as well as composer, arranger, and social critic) eyed the notes on his laptop before diving into a fun and eclectic set of jazz that was lively and spirited. Many faces in the audience that first appeared dull immediately lit up once the band began.

“Unbeknownst to you all, I’m going to sing. Please keep all the animals under control,” Bryon warned the audience before turning to Michael Occhipinti. “You got your twang together?” he asked. “We need that Twang?” The two embarked on a playful and intentionally wobbly rendition of Hank Williams’ "Lovesick Blues.” His chemistry with Occhipinti, an eight-time JUNO Award nominee, was infectious and a pleasure to watch.

There is a focus to Byron, whether playing music or listening to it, that is inspiring. He really appears to listen and take pleasure in the music, particularly when he’s listening to the other musicians play. It’s the mark of a generous musician and a good teacher.

One of the band's final songs of the set Bryon prefaced by explaining that, “On first hearing it, I considered it truly muzak,” (muzak refers to generic commercial background music created for public spaces, aka elevator music). “Turns out to be a tune from Ghana, Guy Warren.” (Guy Warren of Ghana — aka Kofi Ghanaba — was one of the first to mix African rhythms with jazz, afro-jazz.) “If you know this tune, you know this tune,” said Bryon ending with a song that had the audience happily clapping along.

I left pleased but I had an important realization - when I think of jazz I think of Ella Fitzgerald, Billie Holiday, Cassandra Wilson, Diana Krall, Dinah Washington and Amy Winehouse to name a few. They’re all vocalists, and female vocalists at that. I prefer vocalists, particularly when I’m seeing a live show. Because of my love of Mr. Miles Davis, I thought I had no biases when it came to jazz - but I do. I also have tremendous room to grow.

I now wondered if I should just head to Molly Johnson's show or stick to my plan of seeing Victor Lewis. My mother was up for anything so we decided to stick to our plans and see Victor Lewis.

Where’s Victor?

Was Victor Lewis playing? I had no idea. Once I returned to the Rex Jazz & Blues Bar I scrambled to a corner so that I could rest my weary legs.

I wound up on a super high chair – thank you kind stranger man (in the pale blue shirt) for helping me unfold it – and towered awkwardly above my mother and the other tables around me. I still didn't move. In my snug spot in the corner I couldn't see the act. On my right were black and white photographs of Billie Holiday, Louis Armstrong and Diana Krall. On the other side was a mirrored corner, which I foolishly mistook for the band a couple times only to find my own reflection staring back at me.

While I sat waiting for the show I pondered my feelings about jazz and place. When I buy tickets for a show, I don’t give much thought to the venue. But atmosphere is a big part of creating the right vibe, energy and excitement around the music and I decided that the Rex may just not be my venue. After a few minutes I was resolute in this conclusion.

Suddenly the room filled with a huge, warm, all-encompassing sound. My ears immediately perked up. It was the kind of music I refer to as "midnight jazz." David Basskin often plays it on my favorite radio show, Jazz.FM91’s "Stolen Moments". I like to listen to it when I’m writing and the only light is the glow from the screen of my laptop. It keeps me awake more than coffee.

My mother looked at me with a smile, “I’m liking this,” she said. “They’re good.” I agreed.

I rose from my seat to take a peek at the band and there was no Victor Lewis. Ryan Oliver explained that due to unforeseen circumstances Lewis was not able to attend. Joel Haynes filled in on drums.

I stayed anyway.

“I’m breaking stereotypes,” said Oliver, chatting to the audience midway through the show. ”They say jazz musicians don’t plan ahead. Well, I brought a silver pen to sign our albums with.” (His new album is called Strive.)

The music was dynamic and the venue felt warmer, cozier and a little more like my kind of place. The music had transformed the venue’s appeal.

The first set flew by and the band ended it with a Dexter Gordon jazz tune called “Cheesecake.” Gordon apparently always introduced it by saying, “This is about something very good to eat." Listening to the band's buoyant jazz was a wonderful ending to the night. I left smiling. I felt good. Maybe the Rex Jazz & Blues bar was my kind of bar — maybe.

But now I was looking forward to the following night when Mavis Staples and Dr. John would take me to the church of gospel and blues.

I was ready!
