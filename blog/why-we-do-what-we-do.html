Why we do what we do

Submitted by Josh Grossman on Thu Dec 20 1:13pm

I'm a bit of a marketing junkie and so check the Adweek blog (Adfreak) somewhat regularly. Sometime last year they posted a list of 10 commercials directed by David Fincher. At the top of the list was this one:

From a purely objective perspective, it's an excellent commercial: great storyline, character development, tension and release - everything you might expect from a director like Fincher. But it got me thinking.

The storyline contained within the commercial is a fairly common trope: following the athlete from his or her first steps to the championship game, overcoming adversity along the way. It's relatable and exciting; audiences seem to understand the amount of work which goes into becoming a professional athlete, and like to share in the glory of an athlete finally achieving his or her goal.

Becoming a professional musician follows much the same storyline. A musician must start early, learning the very basics or the craft and, over the years, work as hard as any other professional to reach perfection on the instrument of choice. There are similar challenges with performance anxiety, successes and failures along the way, and a fair share of adversity in a variety of forms. And then, finally, the big game is won, the goal achieved - whether it's the performance of a new work, or a sell-out crowd, or a completed album - the sense of satisfaction is enormous.

It's been almost 25 years since I first pressed a trumpet mouthpiece to my lips, and in that time I've experienced some pretty fantastic highs and some pretty disappointing lows. (Let's not talk about that ill-advised attempt at Brandenburg 2 in high school...) There have been solo competition wins and losses; audition successes and failures; performances in front of audiences both packed and meagre. Nothing quite compares to the feeling of taking an idea from infancy to completion, no matter how crazy: booking Kurt Elling to sing with my big band and selling 600 tickets; having a room full of Radiohead fans sing along to my arrangement of "Karma Police"; recording, mixing, mastering, pressing and distributing CDs...These are my World Series trophies, my Grey Cups, my Stanley Cups (minus the strikes, of course).

Every year around this time, as work winds down for a couple of weeks, I'm sure to be thankful for everything I've got. Friends, family and jobs, for sure; but also the people with whom I get to regularly make music (the various musicians who fill the 18 seats of the Toronto Jazz Orchestra, the Chris Hunt Tentet) and whose music I get to enjoy in one way or another.

Our audiences and paycheques are perhaps not as big as those professional athletes so often featured on screen and in print, but our prizes are just as sweet.

Happy holidays.

Josh
