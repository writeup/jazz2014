Gregory Porter turns his audience into "Beliebers!"

Submitted by Jesse Daniel Cole on Sat Jun 29 1:24pm

The scene inside the Enwave Theatre Thursday night was less reminiscent of a jazz performance than it was of a Justin Bieber concert. The crowd was filled with girlish screams and whistles and an air of swooning excitement as the acclaimed Gregory Porter was set to take the stage.

Porter, a jazz and soul singer based out of Brooklyn, New York, found fame with the release of his debut album Water in 2010 and the followup Be Good in 2012. This year’s Liquid Spirit finds him departing from his previous indie label and making the leap into the legendary catalogue of Blue Note artists. He has been praised by publications such as the Huffington Post, Jazz Times magazine and even the Japanese Jazz Life magazine as one of the most important new figures in jazz and soul. The Huffington Post heralded him as the “brilliant new voice of jazz,” while Jazz Wise referred to him as “...a voice to die for.”

Porter took the stage at 7:15 p.m. dressed in avocado green jeans, a white sports jacket and his signature hat. He wasted no time moving into “Painted on Canvas,” a song about “mutual respect” as he put it, which tells us we should allow each other to paint our own canvases. By the end of that first tune, parts of the crowd were already giving standing applause to the singer and his band.

Musically, Porter and his backing band were vice-grip tight, which makes sense since the band has remained the same throughout the past three albums. Bassist Aaron James and drummer Emanuel Harrold held the set together with their ability to jump from ballad to up-tempo numbers without a second’s thought. James and Harrold showed tact and understanding of their place in the band, never seeking the spotlight but allowing their playing to hold its own. The band’s alto saxophonist, Yosuke “Juice” Sato, was a focal point of many of the songs with his spastic solos that occasionally harkened back to Coltrane’s playing on Stellar Regions. The band’s pianist and musical director, Chip Crawford, proved to be yet another focal point of the show, playing rambunctiously, at one point even playing the strings inside the piano by hand.

Of course, Porter stole the show, engaging the audience on many of his songs by inviting the eager crowd to join in clapping and singing along with “No Dying Love” and “Liquid Spirit.” He delivered a rousing rendition of “On My Way to Harlem,” a song about the borough’s influence on Porter, who was originally born in Los Angeles, CA, but credits much of his musical influences as being from Harlem. With the lyric “You can’t keep me away from where I was born, I was baptised by the sound of my daddy’s horn,” perhaps Porter is not speaking literally but rather metaphorically in the sense of his musical self being born and baptised in jazz from the artists of the Harlem scene, artists such as Duke Ellington and poet Langston Hughes (both artists are mentioned specifically in the song). The song is a perfect example of Porter’s ability to sing with a classic jazz sensibility while still breathing an air of contemporary music into his composition and performance. The song fades away with the lyric “Hey, there goes that A-Train...”

By the end of the show, Porter’s amalgamation of new and old proved a hit not just among the hardcore Belieber-style fans, but crossed over to older generations of jazz fans. One of the few truly exceptional jazz singers today, Porter has all the makings of a timeless artist.

Jesse Daniel Cole
