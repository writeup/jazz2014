George Benson: Timeless Talent 

Submitted by Erica Rae Chong on Wed Jun 27 4:51pm

By Erica Rae Chong 

In 1976, George Benson rocketed to fame with the pop-jazz album Breezin'. The title track's easygoing melody has survived the ensuing decades, becoming immortalised as a smooth jazz standard.

Thirty-six years on and the only things challenged by time are Benson’s thinning hair and bigger waistline. The 10-time Grammy Award winner, who turns 70 next year, proved he still has the chops, during a high energy showcase of skill and soul at the Toronto Star Stage Tuesday night. 

Benson, decked out in a snazzy silver-and-black striped vest over a black shirt and pants, launched into the beloved “Breezin’” riff with gusto, fingers as spry as they were all those years ago. He was barely a couple of bars into the song before the audience’s hoots and applause threatened to drown him out. Some fans waved a homemade sign plastered with pictures of the performer.

The six-piece band was in top form, delivering crowd-pleasers such as, “Turn Your Love Around” and "Nothing's Gonna Change My Love For You." Benson’s soulful vocals on the latter had the audience singing along to the chorus — a real lighters-in-the-air moment. 

The group kept the momentum with an upbeat rendition of "At The Mambo Inn". L.A. Keyboardist David Garfield served up an impressive solo, hammering at the keys with incredible speed and invigorating the piece with a boogie-woogie vibe. At times, however, the loud keyboard overwhelmed the rest of the band, including Benson’s intricate guitar strains.

The evening took on a more mellow tone when Benson delivered an instrumental cover of “Don’t Know Why.” Stripped of his usual fast-fingered flourishes, the player’s simple plucking resulted in a fresh take on the tune popularized by Norah Jones.

Benson recounted recording that song for his current album Guitar Man during the wee hours at a small recording studio in a dodgy Israeli neighbourhood.  "I bet you didnt know there's a ghetto in Israel," he said. "When we gave the cabbie the name of the place we wanted to go, he said 'Oh, no! I ain't going there. Nobody goes there this time of night.' We had to pay the cabbie double the fare to get there!"

Benson threw in a few more instrumental pieces such as "Affirmation"and "Tequila", also from Guitar Man, and impressed the crowd with his impeccable finger work on his classic Ibanez guitar. Whistles erupted each time his fingers flew across the fret board, getting louder with each song. 

By the end of the night, Benson had the primarily middle-aged crowd on their feet and dancing to the R&B favourite "Give Me The Night".

With a simple bow, the Pittsburgh native and his entourage left the stage to a roaring standing ovation. The band soon returned with Benson, now clad in a white jacket, for a two-song encore, beginning with a tender "Since I Fell For You".

"Now, we got to go, but we gon' play what we came here to play,” said Benson. Cheers flooded the packed tent in anticipation of the final song, fans knew what was coming. Benson delivered an extended version of his trademark tune “On Broadway”, embellished with tongue-twisting scat singing, a rocking drum solo and plenty of Benson’s signature guitar licks.

He thanked the audience with outstretched arms and hinted that a return concert dedicated to the legendary Nat King Cole’s repertoire was in the works.

Between his deft guitar work and buoyant sound, Benson demonstrated that he truly is the “Guitar Man”.

Erica Rae Chong is pursing a bachelor’s degree in Journalism at the University of Toronto Scarborough
